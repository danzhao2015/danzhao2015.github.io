Higher education

A look at college affordability, popular majors and what happens after graduation

By Dan Zhao

Education has substantial impact on both a personal and social level. It is crucial to identify the emerging trends and new landscapes in post-secondary education. Which state has the largest number of colleges and universities, and which state is home to the most expensive university to attend? How do these variables impact the education level of local residents? What is the most popular major in college now and how did the popularity change over time? What are the money-making majors? These questions are worth further scrutiny and the results could be surprising.

In 2016-17, average tuition and fee prices for in-state students at public four-year institutions range from $5,060 in Wyoming and $6,360 in Florida to $15,450 in Vermont and $15,650 in New Hampshire. The average among the 10 states with the highest in-state tuition and fees is $16,536. With a price difference of $22,390, Michigan has the biggest gap between in-state tuition and out-of-state tuition, followed by $21,370 in Vermont and $20,120 in Hawaii.

California is home to 1,246 institutions, more than any other U.S. state. In second is New York (632), followed by Pennsylvania (544), Texas (506) and Florida (439). On the other hand, Wyoming only has 17 institutions, Delaware (23), North Dakota (30), and Vermont (32). The map above shows the density of institutions in the U.S.

Granted that California has the largest number of institutions among all the U.S. states, it only ranks 15th (40.6 percent) on educational attainmant level, which is measured by the percentages of persons with a bachelor’s degree or higher. According to the U.S. Department of Education, the most educated state is Distinct of Columbia, with 76.5 percent of people holding a bachelor’s degree or higher. Next is Massachusetts (54.6 percent), Maryland (51.7 percent), Connecticut (51.1 percent), and Colorado (48.6 percent). The bottom three are West Virginia (24 percent), Arkansas (25 percent) and Mississippi (26.7 percent).

As of 2015, the top three most popular bachelor’s degree majors are business, health professions and related programs, and social sciences and history. Psychology, visual and performing arts, education, communications, and biological and biomedical sciences have similar numbers of degrees conferred. The least popular majors are precision production, library science, and military technologies and applied sciences.

On a historical scale, the number of bachelor’s degrees conferred witnessed dramatic fluctuations before 2000. The number of business and computer sciences and engineering majors reached a pick during 1985-1986, but then plummeted immediately after the upward trend. Most fields of study have been slightly decreasing since 2000, except natural sciences and mathematics, as well as computer sciences and engineering.

When it comes to specific major, several majors have been rising sharply since 2000. These majors include, for example, agriculture and natural resources, biological and biomedical sciences, communication and journalism, engineering, health professions, mathematics, and psychology. It is also noteworthy that a considerable amount of majors dropped dramatically in year 2011-12. The global financial crisis of 2007-2008 might be one of the factors that lead to the downturn in higher education. Computer and information sciences was the only major that kept growing while the majority of the majors were decreasing.

Unsurprisingly, the highest earning majors are the STEM majors; more specifically, the “E” of STEM — Engineering. Eight out of the ten highest-paying college majors are in engineering fields, such as computer engineering and electrical engineering. The median annual earnings of engineering majors can be as high as more than $70,000. In contrast, the lowest-paying majors such as early childhood education only earn half of that amount.

There are significant gender differences in education level and employment outcomes. Women are more likely to pursue a higher degree, whereas men are more likely to end up with a high school degree. It is obvious that a higher degree brings higher annual income. However, income inequality in employment has been a severe problem. Given the same education level, the median annual income of men has always been higher than women.
